2019 · Behavioural Science · London

PwC

Applying behavioural science hypothesis frameworks to BI tool workflows — designing interventions that measurably shifted how data scientists and accountants worked.

Role
UX Behavioural & Data Research Lead
Client
PwC
Approach
Behavioural Science · Hypothesis-Led Design · Adoption Tracking
Context

Most UX projects improve usability. This one had to change behaviour.

PwC engaged me to redesign BI tool workflows used by two very different user groups: data scientists who lived inside the tools and understood their complexity, and accountants who needed reliable outputs without the overhead of learning a technical system.

The brief went beyond standard usability improvement. The measure of success wasn’t whether users could complete tasks — it was whether they chose to. Adoption was the metric. Behaviour change was the design problem.

The Approach

Hypothesis-led design — treating UX like a scientific experiment.

I applied a Behavioural Science hypothesis framework to the redesign process — treating each design intervention as a testable hypothesis with a predicted effect on a specific adoption indicator.

Before designing anything, I mapped the friction points that were causing users to abandon the tools or work around them entirely. Each friction point was analysed through a behavioural lens: is this a capability barrier (they can’t do it), a motivation barrier (they don’t want to), or a prompt barrier (they don’t think to)? Each type of barrier requires a fundamentally different design response.

This framework meant every design decision had a stated purpose and a measurable expected outcome — rather than the more common pattern of redesigning based on aesthetic preference or assumed best practice.

Redesigning a tool is easy. Changing whether people choose to use it is the harder, more interesting problem.

Interventions & Outcomes

Designing for the moments that shape habits.

The interventions targeted specific behavioural inflection points: onboarding sequences designed to build early confidence, workflow shortcuts surfaced at the moments users were most likely to abandon, and output formats redesigned to match the mental models of accountants rather than data engineers.

User adoption indicators were tracked before and after each intervention — giving a measurable signal of which design changes were actually shifting behaviour and which were cosmetic. This feedback loop informed a second round of refinements.

The result was a redesign grounded in evidence rather than assumption — and a set of documented behavioural hypotheses that PwC could apply to future tool rollouts.

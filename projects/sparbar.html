SPAR
BAR

UX and product design for an innovative reactive boxing training system — bridging physical hardware design with digital product thinking.

Role
Product & UX Designer
Type
Hardware + Digital Product
Sector
Sports Technology · Consumer
The Product

Where physical product design meets digital UX.

Sparbar is a reactive boxing training system — a freestanding target that responds to strikes, building reflexes, timing, and combinations in a way that static bags can’t. The design challenge was not purely digital: the physical product and the digital experience around it needed to be designed as a unified whole.

This kind of project sits at the intersection of industrial design thinking and UX — the physical interaction model shapes what the digital layer can and should do, and vice versa.

Design Approach

Designing for a user in motion.

The primary user context was unusual from a UX perspective: someone in the middle of physical exertion, wearing gloves, operating on instinct rather than deliberate interface interaction. Standard UX assumptions — careful reading, precise tapping, sustained attention — don’t apply.

This shaped every design decision: feedback systems needed to be immediate and peripheral, not requiring the user to stop and look at a screen. Progress indicators needed to be glanceable. Any digital interaction needed to be designed for use in 30-second windows between rounds.

The design work also covered the product’s market positioning — how does a piece of training equipment communicate its innovation and quality to a consumer audience that ranges from serious amateur boxers to fitness enthusiasts who’ve never stepped in a ring?

Designing for a user mid-round, gloves on, heart rate at 160 — that’s a UX constraint that changes everything about how you approach the interface.

Outcome

A product designed for the real conditions of use.

The design work produced a product experience grounded in the actual conditions of use — not a gym in a controlled research environment, but someone alone in their garage at 6am, working through combinations. The best products are designed for the real version of the user, not the idealised one.
